\section{Other symbols}
%%%
The following symbols are used for the illustration of linguistic changes.
%%%
\begin{tabbing}
\TABh \= \kill
\textasciitilde \> variant\\
<  \> borrowing\is{borrowing}\\
$\leftarrow$  \> derivation\is{derivation} or other synchronic process\\
$\Leftarrow$  \> grammaticalization\is{grammaticalization} or other diachronic process\footnotemark\\
\end{tabbing}

\footnotetext{Note that the term \emph{grammaticalization} is used for different types of linguistic changes leading to re-analysis\is{re-analysis} of a given construction's grammatical meaning. A prototypical instance in this rather broad sense of grammaticalization is the morphologization\is{morphologization} of a formerly lexical morpheme to a grammatical morpheme, as the development of definite markers\is{definite marker} from anaphoric pronouns in Germanic\il{Germanic languages} languages, e.g.~English\il{English} \textit{the (house)} (\textit{the} $\Leftarrow$ Old English\il{Old English} \textit{þæt}) and Swedish\il{Swedish} \textit{(hus)-et} (\textit{-et} $\Leftarrow$ Old Norse\il{Old Norse} \textit{hið}).}
